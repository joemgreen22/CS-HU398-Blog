The SOC Visibility Triad and the Future of Security Tools for Cyber Security

 

In today’s world one of the most looming threats to a business is a cyber-attack. This could include anything from a ransomware attack, discovering malware on your network, your web services being taken down by a DDoS attack, or you losing control of your network because the wrong employee opened a phishing email.  

Luckily companies have developed numerous strategies to not become the victims of cybercrime. Companies typically go one of two routes. Fist they can develop an inhouse Security Operations Center (SOC) to monitor and defend your network. Since this is particularly expensive companies without the technical knowhow or large security budgets will outsource to a Managed Security Service Providers (MSSP) for management of their security services. 

 

The Strategy:

The  ultimate goal of any security team will be to mitigate Risk. To understand this let’s first define “Risk.”

Risk is the potential damage from threats that exploit vulnerabilities. This can be calculated with the formula:

Risk = Threats * Vulnerabilities

 

Where vulnerabilities and threats are defined as:

Vulnerabilities are weak spots within your environment and your assets. This is looking at what could happen, where a threat can penetrate and environment. To give an analogy if we had a castle with walls, this would be any holes in the walls an attacker could enter by. 

Threats are incidents or events that have the potential to exploit a vulnerability and do damage to the environment. Continuing with our analogy we can visualize this as the attackers of the castle. 

So to eliminate risk we will have to either eliminate Threats, which is perceived to be impossible. There will always be a bad actor wanting to exploit your environment. Otherwise, we will have to eliminate Vulnerabilities. While this was a popular strategy historically, there are simply too many endpoints and too many new vulnerabilities to detect and patch them all. While reducing any threats and vulnerabilities is still a useful exercise. The cyber security industry has accepted that risk is inherent, and they are going to have to move beyond a “prevention-only ideology” to provide adequate security. 

To continue our analogy, lets accept that the attackers are going to make it in our castle walls. What now? Well, we are going to have to know where they are and if they are inside. We need alarms and scans of the castle to know if there are any bad actors are inside. Then we are going to need a quick reaction force to kick them out and patch whatever hole in the wall they used to gain access. This new strategy of early detection and accelerated response is the foundation of modern cyber security.

 

The Tools of the Trade:

Companies have come to accept that their budgets need to include a large allocation for cyber security, and those security teams have developed a strategy to defend the network. However, we are going to need tools to implement this plan. A good place to start when looking for security products is the SOC Visibility Triad. The core concept of the Triad is a “early detection and accelerated response” strategy and this is supported by three pillars of the triad, being SIEM, NDR, and EDR.

  • SIEMs (Security Information and Event Management) are tools to analyze logs, user and entity behavior through the network. This data can then be extracted in human readable reports. Giving the security team unique insight into what is happening on their network.
  • NDRs (Network Detection and Response) are tools to manage and analyzing network traffic. This can be used to generate a baseline of normal network behavior. So when suspicious network traffic patterns emerge, they can be quickly responded to and resolved.
  • EDRs (Endpoint Detection and Response) are tools to analyzing the endpoints themselves. They will continuously monitor and collect endpoint data. Then allow the user to take response action such as isolating an endpoint. The more advanced solutions have rules-based automated response and analysis capabilities. 

By leveraging these core security products outlined by the SOC Visibility Triad. The security team gains an overall view of the most critical activity across the entire environment. As well as access to important clues to the earliest and strongest indicators of a pending compromise.
 

 

Looking to the Feature:

Although the “prevention only ideology” has mostly been replaced with an “early detection and accelerated response strategy” based in the SOC Visibility Triad. The cyber security industry is constantly evolving and there are new technology management systems that are becoming more popular. One of these new technologies is XDR (Extended Detection and Response). This is an evolution of the EDR system. XDR is grounded in EDR, unifying it with other security tooling to give security analysts visibility, high-efficacy detection, and more-effective correlation, investigation, and response. While EDR was effective it lacked the ability to match endpoint telemetry with security data from other parts of the environment. This shortcoming was commonly filled by a SIEM, or a homegrown data lake.

XDR is exciting to many security teams because it promises to correlate attack telemetry across tools and devices into a single incident alert. Which is a radical shift from working each alert as an independent incident. This will allow analysts to get a wholistic view of the issue. Additionally, XDR has the potential to uses security analytics to automate root-cause analysis. This would allow the analyst to know what caused the incident at first glance, drastically reducing the time to resolve the vulnerability. Then XDR should automate response actions. If there is ever a security incident in the system, there will be prebuilt response actions lined out to quickly implement. Finally, XDR will lower the barrier to threat hunting and enables it across tools. This will be done by integrating tools like the MITRE ATT&CK framework to make hypothesis-driven threat hunting more accessible.

 

The cyber security industry is seeing increasing demand across the world. This makes sense as humanity is moving to a more online and cloud-based world. With this we will see the cyber security industry continue to evolve and change and bad actors continue to find new ways to exploit systems.
